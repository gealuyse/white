The portfolio as a design problem

Every design decision made on this site, documented as a case study. What I tried, what I chose, and why — applied to the artifact you're reading right now.

Project type Self-directed
Audience Hiring managers, designers, potential collaborators
Constraint Pure static HTML/CSS. No frameworks.
01 — Overview

What is this, exactly?

A portfolio is a design problem. You have a user (the recruiter, the hiring manager, the curious peer), a job to do (evaluate fit), and a system with real constraints (reading time, attention, trust). Most designers treat it as a presentation. I treated it as a product.

This page documents the decisions made. Not to show off the process — but because the reasoning is the evidence. If you want to see how I think about design problems, this is one.

the product is also the case study
02 — Situation

The brief

I needed a portfolio that could communicate design ability to someone who may have 30 seconds — or 30 minutes. That showed real work without obscuring the signal. That had a clear voice without turning it into a personality showcase.

Core tension

Portfolio sites optimized for first-impression aesthetics often hide the actual design thinking. Sites optimized for depth are skipped by time-constrained readers. How do you serve both?

03 — Key decisions

The hard choices

Decision 1: Thesis-led vs work-led opening

Work first

Lead with project thumbnails. Let the work speak immediately. No intro text, no thesis statement.

Thesis first ← chosen

"I design complex systems people can use with confidence." One sentence. Then the work. The sentence frames what follows.

The thesis creates a filter. Someone looking for a visual designer will know immediately this isn't the right fit. Someone looking for a systems designer knows to keep reading. Clarity serves everyone, even when it narrows the audience.

Decision 2: Problem headline vs project name

Project name

"Customer Data Platform" — what it is. Clear, searchable, professional.

Problem headline ← chosen

"Complex logic that couldn't be simplified" — what was hard about it. Gives the reader a reason to keep reading before they know the project name.

The project name communicates what was built. The problem headline communicates what was designed. Those are different things. The second is more interesting to the audience I want to reach.

Decision 3: Annotation system

The handwritten annotation system (draw-in on scroll, .anno-mark + SVG path draw via CSS stroke-dashoffset) was kept from the original design. The reasoning:

Why keep it

The annotations communicate that design is a thinking process, not just an output. They carry voice without adding copy. They're a visual signal that someone made choices — the same signal a portfolio is supposed to transmit.

04 — Constraints

What I worked within

Pure static HTML/CSS. No frameworks, no build tools. Everything had to be interpretable as plain text. The constraint forced discipline — if something couldn't be done in CSS, it probably shouldn't be done at all.

Warm paper palette only. No jarring rebrands or visual pivots. The existing identity — off-white paper, warm ink, faint rules — had to remain continuous across old and new pages.

Laws of UX spacing. Proximity over dividers. Whitespace to signal grouping. No hairlines where whitespace can do the job. This is the spacing discipline the site runs on.

05 — Reflection

What this site doesn't do well

Mobile. The two-column layouts compress rather than gracefully adapt. This is a known tradeoff — prioritized desktop because that's where portfolios are evaluated in most hiring contexts. Not a great excuse. A real issue.

Depth vs speed. The case studies are long. For a recruiter with 45 seconds, even a well-structured case study is too much. The solution (a "recruiter mode" toggle, a TL;DR at the top) is a prototype I haven't shipped.

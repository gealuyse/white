A — Layout & Structure 01–05
01 Reverse order Work first, no hero text. The portfolio opens with project 01 — full bleed. You learn who made it after you've seen what they made. Open →
02 Two-pane fixed Left column stays fixed: project list always visible. Right column shows expanding content. No page navigation. Open →
03 Stack of cards Projects are physical cards stacked on top of each other. Scroll peels the top card away, revealing the one beneath. Open →
04 One long document No section gaps. Everything flows as one continuous piece — like reading an actual brief from start to finish. Open →
05 Full-bleed entry Each project fills the full viewport. Scroll moves you from one "room" to the next. Work is the space, not a list inside it. Open →
B — Work Presentation 06–11
06 Situation first Problem statement before project identity. The name is hidden. You read the constraint before you know whose work it is. Open →
07 One decision Each project shows only the one decision that was hardest to make. That's the whole entry. Link goes deeper if you want more. Open →
08 Locked vs unlocked Each project shows what couldn't change (constraints) and what could (design space). The tension between them is the work. Open →
09 Before / after Project covers have a toggle. Flip between the before state and the after. Impact shown before anything is read. Open →
10 What didn't ship A section showing directions that were explored and rejected. The reasoning for what was cut is as revealing as what stayed. Open →
11 Metrics first Outcome numbers appear before the project title. 1,000,000+ users · Shipped · 3 months. Then the name. Then the story. Open →
C — Interaction & Motion 12–15
12 Blur to focus Project covers start blurred and desaturated. As you scroll into them, they sharpen and come to full colour — like focusing attention. Open →
13 Inline expand Click a project and the case study expands in place — no new page. The homepage never empties. Everything lives in one scroll. Open →
14 Annotation on hover Hover over specific phrases and a handwritten note appears at that point. The annotation system becomes an active interaction. Open →
15 Annotation nav No navigation bar. Handwritten arrows on the page lead you to each section. The annotation system IS the navigation. Open →
D — Visual Direction 16–20
16 Dark paper Inverted warm palette: deep off-black background, paper-coloured text. The annotation ink becomes amber. Same editorial feel, different weight. Open →
17 Field notes Site feels like a design notebook. Ruled-line background, tab markers on the right, page numbers. Structure that implies working documents. Open →
18 Process artifacts Show design ephemera alongside the work: sticky notes, comment screenshots, annotated briefs. Proof of process, not just outcomes. Open →
19 Diagram hero The hero isn't a project cover — it's a system diagram or conceptual sketch. Shows how you think, not just what you shipped. Open →
20 With portrait A real photo integrated into the hero with a handwritten note pointing to it. Makes the site feel personal, not just a work archive. Open →
E — Content & Meta 21–25
21 Reader modes Toggle between Recruiter mode (outcomes + timeline, 30 seconds) and Designer mode (full process, detailed). Same content, different depth. Open →
22 Honest headers Section names replaced with: "What I'm actually good at" / "What I'm still figuring out" / "Work I won't take on." Radical transparency. Open →
23 Conversation opener The hero isn't a self-introduction. It opens with a direct question to the reader. You introduce yourself after the reader has answered. Open →
24 Portfolio as case study The portfolio IS the case study. The page documents itself: why each section exists, what was cut, what was hard. Meta proof of process. Open →
25 Say less Ultra-minimal: one sentence, three project titles, one contact link. Nothing else. The restraint is the statement. Open →

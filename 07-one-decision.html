01 Customer Data Platform — Segment & Condition Builder Full case study →

The hardest decision

Should the logic builder let users type freely, or guide them through fixed steps?

Option A — Free-form input

Let users type SQL-like expressions. Maximum power, minimum constraints. Familiar to technical users.

Option B — Guided builder ← chosen

Structured dropdowns: attribute → operator → value. Constrained but readable. Non-technical users could own their segments.

↑ What shipped

Why: The users were marketing teams, not engineers. They needed to understand their own segments at a glance — not reconstruct them from logic. Free-form expressions are powerful but they don't communicate their meaning to the next person who reads them. The builder made the logic visible without making it simplified.

02 3BB Member App & Website Full case study →

The hardest decision

Redesign the entire app, or work inside the existing structure and ship only what changed?

Option A — Full redesign

Start from scratch. Clean structure, modern patterns. Solve every problem at once.

Option B — Surgical changes only ← chosen

Identify exactly which screens were touched by the migration. Design only those. Leave the rest as-is.

↑ What shipped

Why: A full redesign would have required rebuilding hundreds of flows that were working fine. The migration only changed specific paths. Scoping to those paths meant we could ship faster, with less regression risk, and without disrupting the 1M+ users who already knew the app.

03 Counter Service POS Terminal View proposal →

The hardest decision

Improve the hierarchy by reorganizing the layout, or by refining what's already there in place?

Option A — Reorganize positions

Move elements to where they logically belong. Better hierarchy. Breaks existing muscle memory.

Option B — Refine in place ← chosen

Keep every element in its existing position. Improve contrast, weight, and size hierarchy without spatial change.

↑ What shipped

Why: Cashiers process hundreds of transactions a day. Their hands know where to tap without looking. Moving elements — even to "better" positions — would have required retraining and introduced errors during the transition. The constraint wasn't the design. It was respecting the users' existing expertise.
